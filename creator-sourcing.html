Creator Sourcing with precision

Creator Sourcing helps brands discover the right creators through AI-powered search. Brands can simply describe their ideal creator in natural language—by niche, audience, geography, creator size, brand affinity, or custom attributes—and receive a curated list of best-match creators.

The beta was launched to a select group of enterprise customers and received strong early validation from brands including Fenty Beauty, Benefit Cosmetics, Fabletics, and Victoria's Secret.

Collaborators Rhea (Designer) Minki (Engineer)
Timeline 4 months
My Role Led the end-to-end design, collaborating closely with Engineering to design within AI constraints and iterating with feedback from Rhea and the product team.

What problem is it solving?

Brand partnerships are one of the highest-leverage yet the most manual thing a marketing team does. Even well-resourced agencies were capping at roughly 20 creator partnerships per month.

The challenge was to identify the best-fit the creator at scale. Existing tools completely broke down the moment brands had specific, contextual criteria like these:

Fenty beauty

"Curly-haired creators for a new curling cream"

Hinge

"LGBTQ creators who met their partner while using Hinge"

Open AI

"College students who use ChatGPT for studying and daily life"

Anthropologie

"Mother-daughter duo creators for mother's day event"

Summary

The project spanned two connected phases:
Discovery - finding the right creator
Vetting - determining whether they're credible, brand-safe, and worth partnering with

The work that mattered most wasn't the interface. It was figuring out how to represent an AI system's uncertainty honestly, without making users distrust results that were actually right. That tension between giving users confidence and being honest about the system's limits, shaped almost every decision in this case study.

Let's dive into it!

Entering prompt to find creators

The team had multiple discovery sessions with different brands to understand how they evaluate creator fit, the types of prompts they would use, and their expectations from the results. These insights helped me design the prompt experience, edge cases, and the system's handling of queries ranging from broad to highly specific.

Reviewing criteria breakdown from the prompt

The prompt is broken down into discrete, evaluable criteria that users can review and prioritize as must-haves or nice-to-haves. AI flags criteria that are either too vague or cannot be reliably evaluated, allowing users to update, or remove them before running the search.

Edge case — AI isn't always right

Criteria that seem clear to users can sometimes be misinterpreted or flagged incorrectly by AI. To avoid creating a frustrating experience, we gave users the ability to override AI recommendations rather than blocking them from proceeding.

Tradeoff

Overridden criteria could lead to low quality results. However, we chose to prioritise user control and trust. A less-than-perfect result is easier to recover from than a system that incorrectly rejects valid intent and prevents users from moving forward.

Reviewing sample creators & giving feedback for better result quality

Before viewing the full results, users review a set of five creator profiles. The flow follows a simple loop:

Review → Approve/Reject → Feedback → Refine Criteria → New Recommendations.

Concern

Each creator rejection triggered another feedback cycle, allowing the system to refine criteria, better understand user intent, and improve recommendation quality. While this helped bridge the gap between what users asked for and what they actually wanted, it also created an endless-loop feeling that left many users frustrated. The team was divided between 2 different approaches.

Approach 1

Let them land in results and learn by doing.. Seeing what their prompt returned teaches them how to ask better than any guided loop would.

Approach 2

Keep showing sample sets until all 5 are approved before the full run commits. More signal → Sharper results → More trust.

The decision — best of both

It was really a Patience vs Building long-term trust debate.

We capped the loop at 3 rounds. After the third set, users get the option to jump to results, with a soft nudge. No block. No guilt. We give them an out while being transparent about the tradeoff.

Getting users to results, even slightly less refined ones, builds more trust than keeping them in a loop that feels endless. This was still a hypothesis at launch, the right threshold needed real usage data to confirm.

Viewing best-matched results

Challenge

The system returns a ranked list of creators evaluated against every selected criterion. Users weren't only asking 'Is this a match?' but rather 'Is this creator worth my time?'

Since discovery relied on the latest 100 ingested posts, AI confidence was only as strong as the available data.

The challenge was to communicate relevance, confidence, and uncertainty through design, so users could make informed shortlisting decisions without digging deeper into every profile.

Solution

After several rounds of exploration and iteration, we arrived at a design that made both relevance and confidence visible at a glance.

  • Match Score + AI Summary → Why it's a match.
  • Criteria Chips → Evidence and confidence behind the match.

Users could now decide not just who to shortlist, and how much trust to place in each recommendation.

Shortlisting creators

Users can organize creators into new or existing lists, creating a natural bridge between discovery and campaign planning.

Challenge

On the shortlist page, we encountered architectural challenges. Because a shortlist could contain creators from multiple searches, each carrying its original criteria, the table had to support every criterion across the list — which meant a wide, sparse interface that was nearly impossible to scan.

Solution

This challenge required complete rethinking the shortlist architecture, which wasn't feasible for the MVP.

Instead, we introduced criteria-based filtering, allowing users to quickly surface creators by Match and No Match status.

Vetting creators

Discovery helped brands find the right creators, vetting helps them confidently assess whether those creators are legitimate, brand-safe, and worth collaborating with. Vetting answers questions like:

"Should have never worked with a competitor brand."
"Do they have any political controversy?"
"Have they spoken negatively about our brand?"
"What is their audience's perception about them?"
"Do they have Fake engagement?"
The challenge

To answer these questions, Vetting expands the analysis to a creator's full profile history, including owned posts, comments, external web mentions, and signals uncovered through reverse searches across our database enabling more confident decision-making.

Each required a different evidence source and level of analysis.

The challenge was to bring these signals together into a single, trustworthy experience without overwhelming users with research they didn't have time to conduct themselves.

Solution

We built vetting on the same natural-language workflow users already knew from discovery.

Findings were organized into two pillars:

  • Flags - performance quality, posting behavior, brand criticism, political commentary, and other risk signals.
  • Competitor Mentions - evidence of relationships with competing brands.

A high-level safety assessment supports rapid evaluation, with detailed evidence available on demand within each creator profile.

Reflections

Navigating tradeoffs

Some of the most important decisions in this project involved competing viewpoints with no obvious right answer. The breakthrough rarely came from choosing one side over the other. It came from staying with the tension long enough to uncover a better alternative.

Understanding the bigger picture

Users were about to pay real people and put their brand's name behind a recommendation, and every design decision was really a decision about how much we were asking them to trust a system that could be wrong. That's a heavier thing to design for than I expected going in.

Design & engineering collaboration

I used to think design and engineering disagreements were about negotiating feasibility, it was really about educating each other on AI and design, and unlocking edge cases neither of us would have thought of individually.
