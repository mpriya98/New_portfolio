Custom report tracking

Custom Reports let brands define exactly what they want to track—whether a gifting campaign, creator partnership, or product launch—and create a persistent dashboard around it. Using Plot's video intelligence, the system continuously surfaces relevant content based on what is visible and spoken in videos, not just captions, hashtags, or brand tags.

What problem it solved

This solved a major visibility gap: marketing teams had no reliable way to track what creators actually posted, especially when content went untagged.

Impact

Brands uncovered 3× more relevant campaign content on average, giving them a complete view of campaign, event, and use-case performance. As a result, report creation increased 8× compared to the previous topic-tracking experience.

Collaborators Rhea (Designer) Lap Nguyen (Engineer)
Timeline 3–4 months
My Role Owned the report exploration experience and redesign of report creation, collaborating with product, engineering and customer success to understand customer pain points and translate them into product improvements.

Overall analytics

The Analytics tab answers the question every marketing team has before a leadership meeting: what's actually performing, and can I stand behind these numbers?

Performance across creator partnerships, organic UGC, and owned content is unified in one view — filterable, comparable, and exportable. Here's what they can dig into:

  • Report summary — AI-generated performance read with a link to the full AI report
  • Overall performance metrics — mentions, views, engagement, and EMV by source (Organic UGC, Creator Partnerships, Owned)
  • Sentiment breakdown — positive, neutral, and negative split with drill-through to the content driving each signal
  • Key insights — auto-surfaced highlights so teams don't have to scan for what matters
  • Platform breakdown — performance by channel
  • Demographics — age, gender, language, and country to understand who the content is reaching

Tracking Creator partnerships

When a brand sends out 50 gifting packages, there's no reliable way to know if the creator posted or what they said about it. Creator and gifting programs become nearly impossible to measure — ROI is invisible, and the only fallback is manually checking each creator's profile.

Creator Partnerships gives that visibility back. Brands add the creators they're working with, and video intelligence picks up every related post they publish — tagged or not.

Onboarding Experience — How we reduced drop-offs

Onboarding experience walked users through a linear flow from describing what they wanted to track, to reviewing everything before setting it up.

Steps included: Add details → Set timeline → Add creators → Add deliverables → Review

The first version was designed and shipped by another fellow designer on the team, but real usage surfaced a set of problems that needed rethinking.

The Problem
  • Drop-offs mid-setup — the steps weren't clear enough, and users abandoned the flow before completing it.
  • Errors with no way forward — when something broke, users couldn't understand why or how to fix it.
  • A blank prompt, too early — describing what to track sounds simple, but most teams didn't know the answer until content was already coming in.

Step 1 — Add details

The first step of the onboarding asked brands to name their report and describe what they wanted to track.

Before

Problem

Brands described what the report should track in a single open text field — no guidance, no guardrails. The AI matched literally. Too broad, and irrelevant posts flooded in. Too specific, and valid content got missed. Feedback only surfaced once data came in. By then, the report had already been running on a broken prompt.

After

Solution

Rather than asking brands to write a prompt from scratch, the redesign replaced the open text field with a guided setup. Structured questions about the campaign, product, content type, what to include or exclude, are fed directly into the AI matching logic. Brands could see exactly what the report would track before it went live, and edit it if something looked off.

Step 2 — Add creators

The Add Creators step was where brands uploaded the creators they were working with — via CSV or manually — so AI knew whose content to track.

Upload CSV to add creators

Problem

The existing flow required brands to upload a CSV of creators. Any mismatch in format, extra columns, or encoding issues caused silent failures — no clear error, no way forward. Most users hit a wall, gave up, and sent the file over Slack asking the team to set it up for them. It became a support bottleneck and a consistent drop-off point in the onboarding flow.

Reviewing added creators

Addressing edge cases

Process

Through customer success conversations, we learned CSV formatting wasn't as intuitive as assumed. Users made mistakes they didn't recognise as mistakes — but the LLM needed data in a specific structure, so small errors broke the pipeline silently.

Solution

We mapped failure points across three layers:

  • File-level formatting
  • LLM detection confidence
  • Manual override errors

The redesign caught errors at each layer — surfacing them early, explaining them clearly, and letting users fix them without starting over.

Step 3 — Add deliverables

The step we kept revisiting — and eventually cut entirely

This step asked brands to define what each creator was contracted to deliver — platform, format, and quantity. The underlying logic was complex: a deliverable could be one Instagram Reel OR one TikTok, or both together.

Challenges
  • Representing AND/OR conditions in a UI that was intuitive proved harder with every iteration — and asking brands to reason about it upfront made it worse
  • The LLM couldn't reliably distinguish contracted deliverables from posts creators made out of goodwill, which undermined accuracy across the board
The call

After multiple rounds of iteration to address these challenges, the step was cut entirely — from onboarding and Creator Partnerships. It was a hard call, with ripple effects across the product.

We realised retaining trust in the product mattered more than shipping a feature that introduced doubt.

As we iterated, steps merged, and the flow tightened around what actually mattered. The final onboarding was leaner — because each round taught us what was essential and what was in the way.

Reflections

Knowing when to stop

The deliverable step went through more rounds than anything else — and the final call was to cut it. Better upfront alignment might have saved time. But without going through it, we wouldn't have uncovered the edge cases, understood the LLM's limits, or seen what ambiguous data was doing to user trust. That time wasn't wasted. It was how we found the line.

Designing for errors

The CSV flow pushed me to design for every possible mistake. At some point, that became a problem in itself — blocking users, adding steps, making the experience feel more fragile than it was. Sometimes the right call is to catch what critically matters and let users find their way through the rest.

Trust in data: a design problem

We saw vague prompts returned irrelevant posts — and users didn't question the prompt, they questioned the product. The deliverable step hit the same wall: the LLM couldn't reliably tell contracted posts from organic ones yet. With AI, the gap between what the system does and what users expect is a design problem. Leave it unaddressed, and trust doesn't recover quietly.
